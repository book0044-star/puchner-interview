Program Briefing

YOU
"Hello, I'm Yang Se-hee, producer of YTN's [The Prompt]."
안녕하세요. YTN [더 프롬프트]의 제작진 양세희 피디입니다.
YOU
"A new wave has begun. Many say the moment when artificial intelligence surpasses human intelligence — the 'singularity' — is almost here. But what will that future actually look like?"
AI라는 새로운 물결이 시작됐고, 인공지능의 지능과 기술이 인간을 초월하는 시기, 즉 '특이점'이 곧 온다고 하는데, 과연 그 미래는 어떨까.
YOU
"Who will benefit from the social, economic, and cultural changes of the AI era? If the economy grows, will that wealth be distributed fairly? Will my job really survive? What kind of future will my children face? We are living through an age full of question marks."
AI 시대에 사회적, 경제적, 문화적 변화들은 누구를 위한 것인가? 경제적 성장이 이루어진다면, 부의 분배는 공평할까? 그리고 나의 직업은 정말 사라지지 않을지, 나의 자녀는 어떤 미래를 맞게 될지, 물음표가 가득한 시대를 통과 중입니다.

그래서 YTN은 [더 프롬프트]를 통해 AI 시대에 어떻게 살아야 하는지, 인류의 미래를 묻고자 합니다.
YOU
"The word 'Prompt' here is not just a command we give to AI. We know the difference between an answer that simply retrieves information, and a real answer that includes process and future."
여기서 '프롬프트(Prompt)'는 AI에게 답을 요구하는 명령어가 아닙니다. '정보 취합의 답'과 '과정과 미래를 포함한 해답'의 차이를 우리는 알고 있습니다.

"This program is expected to reach opinion leaders — people who care deeply about global issues and new ideas. It is truly an honor to have you with us today."

Section 1 — Intro Questions

A World-Class Scholar's
Relationship with AI

Light warm-up questions to ease in. Keep answers short and casual. Framing note: "These are lighter questions — please feel free to answer briefly."
가벼운 질문이니, 짧고 편하게 대답해 주시면 좋겠다.

Q1

How do you define AI?

당신은 AI를 어떻게 정의하나?
▼
Expected Answer

"I think of AI as a very powerful new tool for processing and generating language — but it's important to be clear about what that means. It's not a thinking being. It's a system that has learned patterns from an enormous amount of human text. In that sense, it's less like a mind and more like a mirror — reflecting back everything we've ever written, but without the lived experience behind any of it."

Q2

What AI tool do you use most these days — and how much do you trust what it tells you?

요즘 가장 많이 쓰는 AI는 무엇인지? 그리고 당신은 AI의 판단을 얼마나 믿나?
▼
Expected Answer

"I use ChatGPT and Claude regularly — mostly for research, brainstorming, and my chatbot philosophy project. As for trust: I've learned to treat it like a very well-read but sometimes unreliable colleague. It can produce what sounds like a perfectly confident answer and be completely wrong. I've encountered hallucinations — fabricated citations, invented historical details. So I verify. The danger is when people don't."

Q3

People say human life is a story of desire and lack — and that imperfection is what makes us compelling. So why do we demand perfection from AI?

인간의 생을 결핍과 욕망의 서사라고도 한다. 결핍이 있는 인간은 매력적이기도 하다. 그런데 — 왜 우리는 AI에게 완벽을 요구할까?
▼
Expected Answer

"That's such a rich question. The greatest stories in human history — Hamlet, Don Quixote, the Odyssey — are all built on failure, desire, loss. A perfect narrator is actually a boring narrator. There's something almost paradoxical about demanding perfection from a tool we want to help us tell stories. What we might actually want from AI is not perfection, but productive imperfection — something that pushes back, surprises us, even gets things wrong in ways that make us think differently."

Storytelling angle

His core lens is storytelling. Bring the conversation back to: "What kind of story does a perfect narrator tell?" — likely not a very interesting one.

→ Transition into Section 2

"These are fascinating tensions. Let's go deeper — because you're someone who thinks about human history in very long arcs. And from that perspective, AI is something genuinely new. Let me ask you about that."

지금까지 정말 흥미로운 이야기를 해주셨어요. 더 깊이 들어가볼게요 — 교수님은 인류의 역사를 아주 긴 호흡으로 바라보시는 분이잖아요. 그 관점에서 AI는 정말 새로운 무언가인데, 그걸 여쭤보고 싶어요.

Section 2 › AI as a New Species Evolution & History
Section 2 — Evolutionary Lens

AI as a New
Intelligent Species?

This section asks Puchner to apply an anthropological and evolutionary lens to AI. Let him draw the historical parallels — Copernicus, Darwin — and connect them to AI's arrival.

Q2

Every living thing on Earth evolves. Homo sapiens were the first to evolve the capacity for thought. But now AI is arriving — something that may think as well as, or better than, us. Can we view AI through the lens of evolution — as the birth of a new intelligent species?

지구상의 모든 생명체는 진화를 한다. 지구에서 '생각'을 하는 생명체로 진화한 건 현인류 호모 사피엔스가 처음이다. 하지만 곧 인간만큼 혹은 인간보다 더 똑똑한 AI가 등장한다고 한다. AI를 진화론의 관점에서 '새로운 지능을 가진 종'의 탄생으로 볼 수 있을까?
▼
Expected Answer

"I'd be careful with the 'species' framing — biological evolution requires bodies, mortality, reproduction, desire. AI has none of those. But as a metaphor, I think it captures something real: something is emerging that will fundamentally change what it means to be human. In that sense, it's less like the birth of a new species and more like the invention of writing — which also changed us profoundly, which also felt, to some, like a threat to what was essentially human."

If he deflects the evolution framing

"Then how would you describe what's happening? Is there a better metaphor from history — something we've seen before?"

Q2–1

Why do humans fear the arrival of something more intelligent than themselves? Can you give us a historical example — like Copernicus or Darwin — of how humans reacted when their central place in the universe was challenged?

인간은 왜 자신보다 더 똑똑한 AI의 등장에 두려움을 느낄까? 역사적 관점에서 인간이 새로운 문명과 학설을 마주했을 때 어땠는지 예로 들어서 설명해 주시면 좋겠다. — 코페르니쿠스의 지동설, 찰스 다윈의 진화론.
▼
Expected Answer

"Think about what happened when Copernicus said the Earth orbits the Sun. The reaction was ridicule, then hostility, then denial — because it displaced humanity from the center of the cosmos. Darwin was even more threatening: not only are we not at the center of the universe, we're not even separate from the animals. Both ideas were met with fear and contempt before they became foundational to how we understand ourselves. AI is doing something similar — suggesting that human intelligence may not be unique. And I think the initial reaction of fear is entirely understandable. But history also shows us that these shifts ultimately expand our sense of what's possible, rather than diminishing us."

Key historical beats he may mention

Copernicus: humans move from center to periphery of the cosmos. Darwin: humans are part of the animal kingdom, not separate from it. AI: humans may not be the sole intelligent beings. Each shift triggered fear, then eventually awe and expansion of perspective.

Q2–2

Throughout history, every great revolution has been accompanied by conflict — and the victors wrote the history. If AI and humanity are in a kind of contest, who will write the history of the future?

인류의 역사를 보면, '새로운 혁명'은 늘 갈등을 동반한다. 그리고 전쟁에서 이긴 자가 새로운 역사를 썼다. 그렇다면 인간과 AI의 전쟁, 그 미래의 역사는 누가 쓸까?
▼
Expected Answer

"History has always been written by those who control the means of telling stories — and for most of human history, that required language, writing, and institutions that preserved them. AI can generate text at a scale no human can match. But history isn't just text. It's the act of assigning meaning — deciding what matters, what to remember, what to forget. That is still a human act. The question I'd ask is: will we remain the ones making those decisions? Because if we outsource that to AI, then yes — the history of this era may be written by something other than us."

→ Transition into Section 3

"The question of who writes history leads us to language — because language is how history gets written at all. And you've spent your career studying exactly that. Let's talk about what happens when AI enters the world of language."

누가 역사를 쓰느냐는 결국 언어의 문제로 이어지잖아요. 교수님이 평생 연구하신 바로 그 주제이기도 하고요. 이제 AI가 언어의 세계에 들어왔을 때 무슨 일이 일어나는지 이야기해볼게요.

Section 3 › Language, LLMs & Culture Core Territory
Section 3 — Language & Power

Language, LLMs
& Cultural Power

This is Puchner's home territory. He's written about the history of writing and how language shapes civilization. Give this room to breathe — his answers here will be the richest.

Q3

Human civilization was built on language, writing, and the recording of knowledge. AI's breakthrough is also built on language — Large Language Models. Why is language so central to both human civilization and to AI?

인간은 '언어'의 발달과 '문자'의 탄생, 그리고 '기록'으로 이어지며 새로운 역사를 썼다. AI 또한 '대형 언어 모델' LLM이 변곡점이라고 얘기한다. AI의 탄생과 성장에 '언어'가 왜 중요한가?
▼
Expected Answer

"Language is the technology that made human civilization possible. Every major leap — from cave paintings to the alphabet, from the printing press to the internet — has been a revolution in how we store and share language. And now LLMs have done something extraordinary: they've processed the entire written record of human civilization and learned the patterns within it. In a sense, they've read everything. That's unprecedented. But here's what's also true: they know our words, but they don't know our lives. They've absorbed the surface of human culture without the lived experience underneath it."

Context he may draw on

The word "algorithm" comes from the name of a 9th-century Persian mathematician, al-Khwārizmī — a reminder that mathematics and language have been intertwined from the start. He may frame AI as the convergence of the humanities and sciences.

Q3–1

Until now, only humans have been storytellers. But AI has gained language. Yuval Harari warned at Davos this year that if AI masters language and creates more compelling narratives than humans, it could reshape power structures and social systems. Do you agree?

지금까지 지구상에 인간만이 '스토리텔러'였다. 하지만 AI가 언어를 갖게 되면서 상황은 달라졌다. 유발 하라리는 세계경제포럼에서 '인공지능이 언어를 장악하여 인간보다 더 뛰어난 서사와 논리를 만들어 낼 수 있다면 권력의 핵심도 바뀌고, 사회 시스템도 바뀔 것이다'라고 경고했다. 그의 주장에 대해서 어떻게 생각하나?
▼
Expected Answer

"Harari is raising an alarm I take seriously. Language has always been power — whoever controls the dominant narrative controls culture. That's been true from sacred texts to newspapers to social media. Now AI can produce language at a scale and speed no human can match. The question is: will the stories AI tells be the stories we've chosen? Or will we find ourselves living inside narratives we didn't author? I think the warning is valid. But I'd also say: humans still decide which stories become authoritative. The danger comes when we stop asking who's making that decision."

Source

Harari spoke at the World Economic Forum in Davos, January 2026. Quote: "When AI controls language, it shapes society."

Q3–2

Language carries more than information — it carries culture and emotion. French is called the language of love; Italian sounds like song; Korean is celebrated for its scientific precision and expressive range. Can AI's language ever carry that kind of beauty? What would it need to become a truly global language?

언어는 단순 정보 전달 외에도 문화와 정서를 담고 있다. 한국어도 아주 과학적인 문자라는 평이 있고, 의성어나 의태어가 많아서 감정 표현이 다양하다. AI의 언어도 시대의 정서를 반영한 아름다움을 담을 수 있을까? AI의 언어가 '시대를 초월한 글로벌 언어'가 되기 위해서 갖춰야 할 것은 무엇일까?
▼
Expected Answer

"AI can absolutely mimic the surface of beautiful language — the rhythm, the syntax, even the emotional register — because it has learned from centuries of beautiful writing. What I find genuinely impressive is its range. But beauty in language isn't just style — it's the trace of a life behind the words. When I read Chekhov, I'm not just reading sentences; I'm encountering a consciousness. The Korean language is a fascinating case — your sound symbolism, the way emotion is woven directly into words, reflects something about how Korean culture experiences the world. AI can learn to imitate that. Whether it can genuinely carry it is a different question."

Korea angle — use this

Mentioning Korean specifically (scientific writing system, rich emotional vocabulary) will resonate with him — he's interested in K-culture and interprets culture as the movement of power. He'll appreciate the specificity.

Q3–3

You've been doing something remarkable — having conversations with historical philosophers through AI chatbots. Socrates, Confucius, the Buddha, Jesus — none of them wrote their own words. What did you discover about AI's potential through that project?

인공지능의 언어를 창의적으로 쓰는 방법에 대해서도 연구 중이시죠? 특히, 챗봇으로 과거의 철학자들과 대화를 시도한 계기가 궁금하고, 그것을 통해서 AI의 언어적 잠재력을 발견하셨는지도 궁금하다.
▼
Expected Answer

"What started me on this project was a simple observation: Socrates, Confucius, the Buddha, Jesus — none of them wrote. They spoke. Their words survive only because disciples wrote them down. So in a strange way, these figures already exist in our world only as text reconstructed by others. That made me curious: what happens when you give that text back to AI and ask it to speak again? What I discovered is genuinely interesting — and genuinely limited. The chatbot Socrates is witty and philosophically coherent. But it never surprises you the way a real person does. It never refuses to answer. It never gets tired. And that absence of limitation is itself a kind of answer about what AI is."

Key insight he may share

LLMs are built on written text — but the great ancient thinkers spoke, not wrote. Their ideas survive through disciples' records. AI reconstructing them reveals a fundamental gap: LLMs can write, but can they speak in the full human sense?

챗봇 철학자와 대화하면서 혹시, 영감을 받은 적도 있는지? 챗봇 철학자와의 대화에서 '환각 현상(잘못된 정보)'를 만났을 때.
▼

"Yes — actually, yes. I came to see the hallucination not as a failure of the tool but as an invitation — a strange, generative accident. In some ways that's the most human thing about AI: its mistakes can be more interesting than its successes."

Section 4 — The Humanities in the AI Age

Philosophy &
the Humanities

Puchner is a passionate advocate for the humanities in the AI era. This section explores what kind of learning survives — and thrives — when AI can write essays and answer questions.

Q4

Those conversations with AI philosophers remind me of ancient Greek academies — people gathered in amphitheaters to debate and think together. In the AI age, do you dream of returning to that original form of learning?

챗봇 철학자들과의 대화는 그리스 로마 시대의 아카데미, 대형 원형 극장의 토론을 연상시킨다. AI 시대에 다시 배움의 원형으로 돌아가는 수업을 꿈꾸는가?
▼
Expected Answer

"Absolutely — and I think AI might actually make that possible again. If AI handles the information retrieval, the summarizing, the first-draft writing, then what's left for human education? Dialogue. Debate. The Socratic method. Sitting across from someone who challenges you, who doesn't accept your first answer, who asks you to go deeper. That's what the ancient academies were doing, and it's the part of education that AI genuinely cannot replace. I dream of a classroom where the textbook is AI and the teacher is a Socratic guide."

Q4–1

You've advocated strongly for the humanities. Yet as of 2021, only 8% of Harvard's incoming class said they were most interested in arts and humanities. As someone who champions this field — why do the humanities matter in the age of AI?

당신은 인문학의 중요성도 강조하고 있다. 2021년 기준, 당신이 재직 중인 하버드 신입생의 8%만이 예술과 인문학에 가장 관심이 있다고 말했다고 한다. AI 시대에 왜 인문학이 중요할까?
▼
Expected Answer

"Eight percent — that number keeps me up at night. And yet I'd argue that the humanities have never been more urgent than they are right now. Because the humanities ask the question that AI cannot answer for us: what does it mean to be human? What do we owe each other? What is a life worth living? These are not decorative questions. They're the questions that determine how we build AI, how we regulate it, how we live with it. An engineer who has never thought about power, or narrative, or the history of how technologies reshape societies — that engineer is flying blind. The humanities are not a luxury. They are the user manual for being human in a technological world."
